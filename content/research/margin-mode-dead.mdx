## The Idea

Our core strategy captures positive funding: buy spot + short perp. When rates are positive, shorts collect.

The mirror: when rates are **negative**, longs collect. So borrow the spot token, sell it, and go long perp. You'd collect negative funding while paying borrow interest.

If this works, the engine never goes idle - positive rates in bull markets, negative rates in bear markets. The theoretical CAGR jumped from 19.55% to ~35-40%.

## The Data

We collected real-time borrow rates from:
- **Bybit:** 231 borrowable coins, 30 days of hourly history
- **Binance:** 435 coins, 7 days of hourly history

Cross-referenced 139 symbols with negative trailing funding against borrow costs:
- **26 viable** after subtracting borrow rates (borrow rate < funding income)
- **113 killed** by: no borrow pool (80+), or borrow rate exceeds funding rate

Top candidates: KAVA (199% net APY), ICX (114%), ANKR (63%), AXS (39%).

## Why It Fails

### The Asymmetry

Positive funding rates are **structurally persistent**. In bull markets, traders pile into leveraged longs, creating sustained demand for short funding. This is the engine's edge.

Negative funding rates are **transient spikes**. They occur during brief panic sells or delevering events, then revert to neutral or positive within hours to days.

### The Cost Floor

When you borrow a token:
- **Borrow cost is stable** - you pay it continuously, every hour, regardless of what funding does
- **Funding income is volatile** - the negative rate that looked attractive yesterday may be neutral today

ME/Bybit example: scoring saw -0.12%/interval trailing rate. Over the hold period, actual rate averaged -0.002% - a 50× drop. Borrow cost: $411. Funding collected: $95.

### The Structural Problem

In traditional spot-vs-perp (positive funding), your costs are fixed (execution fees) and your income is persistent (funding from overleveraged longs).

In margin mode, this inverts: your costs are persistent (borrow interest) and your income is transient (brief negative funding spikes). The math doesn't work.

## Could It Ever Work?

Possibly, with:
1. **Extreme-only entry** - only enter when negative rate exceeds 5× borrow cost
2. **Sub-day holds** - exit within hours, before mean-reversion kills the rate
3. **As a signal, not a strategy** - extreme negative funding may predict short-term price bounces

None of these are high priority. The core strategy (positive funding, spot-vs-perp) produces 19.55% CAGR without this complexity.

## Conclusion

**Strategy status: DEAD.**

Negative funding ≠ inverse of positive funding. The asymmetry between structural persistence (positive) and transient spikes (negative) makes the mirror strategy fundamentally unprofitable with current entry filters.
